# 七転び八起き (Nana korobi ya oki): Fall Down Seven Times, Get Up Eight

**Nana korobi ya oki** (七転び八起き) is a famous Japanese proverb that translates to **"Fall down seven times, get up eight."**

It is the ultimate expression of **resilience, perseverance, and the refusal to give up.** The proverb emphasizes that no matter how many times you are knocked down by adversity or failure, you must always find the strength to get back up.

---

### The Core Philosophy

The number "seven" is used here not as a literal count, but to represent "many" times. The number "eight" symbolizes "one more time." The message is simple but profound:

*   **Failure is Inevitable:** Life will knock you down. You will face setbacks. This is a given.
*   **Getting Up is a Choice:** While falling may be unavoidable, getting back up is a conscious act of will.
*   **The "Eighth" Time is What Matters:** Your final outcome is not defined by how many times you fall, but by your commitment to rise one more time than you are knocked down.

This proverb is a cornerstone of the Japanese concept of **Ganbaru** (doing one's best) and is deeply embedded in the culture's emphasis on persistence and fortitude.

---

### Connection to Other Concepts

*   **Resilience:** Nana korobi ya oki is the very definition of a resilient mindset. It accepts setbacks as part of the process and focuses on the recovery.
*   **Gaman (我慢):** The act of getting up the eighth time often requires Gaman—enduring the difficulty with patience and dignity.
*   **Kishi Kaisei (起死回生):** A dramatic "get up the eighth time" moment, where a final act of perseverance turns a hopeless situation into a victory, is a Kishi Kaisei event.
*   **Growth Mindset:** This proverb aligns perfectly with a growth mindset, which views failure not as a final verdict, but as a learning opportunity on the path to mastery.

---

### How to Apply Nana korobi ya oki in Your Life

*   **Reframe Failure:** When you experience a setback, see it as the "first fall," not the final outcome. The story isn't over yet.
*   **Focus on the Next Step:** After a failure, don't dwell on what went wrong. Instead, ask yourself: "What is the one small action I can take to get back up?"
*   **Cultivate Persistence as a Skill:** View perseverance not as an innate trait, but as a muscle you can build. Each time you get back up, you are making that muscle stronger.
*   **Celebrate the "Getting Up":** Acknowledge and take pride in your own resilience. The act of rising after a fall is a victory in itself.

Nana korobi ya oki is a powerful reminder that our strength is not measured by our ability to avoid falling, but by our unwavering commitment to stand up again, every single time.
