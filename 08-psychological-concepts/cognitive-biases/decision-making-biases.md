# Decision-Making Biases

Our decision-making is often influenced by irrational shortcuts. This guide covers biases that specifically affect our choices and behaviors.

| Bias | Description | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Authority Bias** | Overvaluing opinions from authority figures. | Believing a supplement works just because a celebrity doctor endorses it. |
| **Contrast Effect**| Perception is altered by comparing with something else. | A room temperature drink feels cold after drinking hot tea. |
| **Information Bias** | Seeking information even when it doesn't affect action. | Wanting extra medical tests even though a diagnosis is confirmed. |
| **Omission Bias** | Judging harmful inactions as less immoral than harmful actions. | A parent avoids vaccinating a child, fearing side effects, despite greater risk. |
| **Endowment Effect** | Valuing things more just because you own them. | Overpricing a used mug just because it's yours. |
| **Confirmation Bias** | Favoring information that confirms existing beliefs. | Only reading news from sources that align with your political views. |
| **Attentional Bias** | Paying more attention to certain stimuli while ignoring others. | Someone anxious about illness constantly notices health-related news. |
| **Focusing Effect**| Overemphasizing one aspect of a situation while ignoring others. | Assuming moving to a sunny city will increase happiness, ignoring job or relationships. |
| **Status Quo Bias** | Preferring the current state and resisting change. | Staying with the same phone provider despite better offers. |
| **Choice-Supportive Bias** | Remembering one's choices as better than they were. | Believing your choice of college was perfect, despite initial doubts. |
| **Outcome Bias** | Judging a decision by its outcome, not its quality. | Praising a risky investment because it made money, even if it was a poor choice. |
| **Zero-Risk Bias** | Preferring options that eliminate risk completely, even when better options exist. | Choosing a treatment that reduces a minor risk to zero over one that significantly reduces a major risk. |
| **Hyperbolic Discounting** | Preferring smaller, immediate rewards over larger, delayed ones. | Choosing $20 today over $50 in a month. |
| **Post-Purchase Rationalization** | Justifying purchases after the fact to reduce buyer’s remorse. | Claiming expensive headphones were necessary for “focus” after an impulse buy. |
| **Bandwagon Effect**| Adopting beliefs or behaviors because others are doing so. | Investing in a stock just because it's trending on social media. |
| **Illusion of Control** | Belief that one can control outcomes they can’t. | Blowing on dice for luck in a casino. |
| **Planning Fallacy** | Underestimating the time or resources needed for a task. | Believing a project will take 2 weeks, but it takes 2 months. |
| **Loss Aversion** | The pain of losing is psychologically twice as powerful as the pleasure of gaining. | Refusing to sell a declining stock to avoid realizing a loss. |
