# ⚔️ Dispute Resolution vs. Deal Making

While all negotiation involves reaching an agreement, the context dramatically changes the strategy. The two primary
contexts are **Dispute Resolution** and **Deal Making**. Understanding which one you are in is crucial for choosing the
right approach.

---

## 😠 Dispute Resolution: Looking Backward to Fix a Problem

**What it is:** Dispute resolution is a negotiation focused on resolving a conflict that has arisen from a 
**past event**. The goal is to find a fair solution to a disagreement, right a perceived wrong, and move on.

**Key Characteristics:**

* **Focus on the Past:** The conversation is centered around what happened, who was at fault, and what is owed.
* **Often Emotional:** Because it stems from a problem, parties often feel wronged, angry, or defensive. Emotions can
  play a significant and disruptive role.
* **Goal is "Fairness" or "Justice":** The aim is to find a solution that feels fair and resolves the grievance. It's
  about getting back to a neutral state.
* **Often a Zero-Sum Game:** It can feel like a win-lose situation, where one party's gain is the other's loss (e.g.,
  the size of a settlement payment).

**Example:**
Two neighbors are negotiating over a tree on one person's property whose roots are damaging the other's driveway. The
discussion is about the past damage and who should pay for the repairs. The emotions are high, and the goal is to find a
fair solution to the existing problem.

---

## 🤝 Deal Making: Looking Forward to Create an Opportunity

**What it is:** Deal making is a negotiation focused on creating a **new agreement or partnership for the future**. The
goal is to build something new together that will provide mutual benefit.

**Key Characteristics:**

* **Focus on the Future:** The conversation is centered around future opportunities, shared goals, and creating value.
* **Generally Less Emotional:** While not devoid of emotion, the tone is typically more optimistic, creative, and
  business-oriented.
* **Goal is "Value Creation":** The aim is to create a "win-win" scenario where both parties are better off than they
  would be without the deal.
* **Often an Integrative Game:** The best deal-makers look for ways to "expand the pie," finding creative solutions that
  give both parties what they want.

**Example:**
Two companies are negotiating a partnership to co-develop and launch a new product. The discussion is about future
responsibilities, revenue sharing, and marketing strategies. The mood is collaborative, and the goal is to create a
successful new venture for both.

---

## 📊 At a Glance: Key Differences

| Aspect             | **Dispute Resolution**                 | **Deal Making**                             |
|:-------------------|:---------------------------------------|:--------------------------------------------|
| **Time Focus**     | Backward-looking (what happened?)      | Forward-looking (what can we create?)       |
| **Core Goal**      | Resolve a past problem; seek fairness. | Create a future opportunity; seek value.    |
| **Emotional Tone** | Often negative, defensive, or angry.   | Often positive, collaborative, or creative. |
| **Mindset**        | "How do we divide the blame/cost?"     | "How do we create and share value?"         |
| **Primary Risk**   | Damaging the relationship further.     | Missing out on a potential opportunity.     |
| **Game Type**      | Tends toward Distributive (Win-Lose).  | Tends toward Integrative (Win-Win).         |

### **Why This Distinction Matters**

Using the wrong approach for the context can be disastrous.

* If you treat a **deal-making** negotiation like a **dispute**, you will be overly adversarial, hide information, and
  miss opportunities to create value, likely killing the deal.
* If you treat a **dispute resolution** like a **deal-making** negotiation, you might be too trusting or fail to
  advocate for your position strongly enough, leading to an unfair outcome.

The first step in any negotiation is to accurately diagnose the situation: Are we here to fix something that's broken,
or are we here to build something new? Your answer to that question should define your entire strategy.
