# Getting Things Done (GTD)

**Getting Things Done (GTD)** is a comprehensive productivity methodology created by David Allen. Its core principle is to get all tasks, ideas, and commitments out of your head and into a trusted external system. By doing so, you free up your mental energy to focus on the task at hand, rather than trying to remember everything you have to do.

**Best for:** Individuals who feel overwhelmed by a high volume of inputs, tasks, and ideas and want a complete system to manage it all.

---

### The 5 Steps of GTD

GTD is a five-step process for managing your workflow.

1.  **Capture:**
    *   **The Idea:** Collect *everything* that has your attention—tasks, ideas, reminders, appointments—into a limited number of "inboxes." This could be a physical tray, a notebook, an email folder, or a digital app.
    *   **The Goal:** Your mind should be for *having* ideas, not for *holding* them.

2.  **Clarify (or Process):**
    *   Regularly process your inboxes. For each item, ask: **"Is it actionable?"**
    *   **If No:** Trash it, file it as a reference, or put it on a "Someday/Maybe" list.
    *   **If Yes:** Decide on the very next physical action required. If it takes less than 2 minutes, do it immediately.

3.  **Organize:**
    *   Put the item where it belongs in your trusted system.
    *   **Projects:** If it requires more than one action, it's a "project." Create a project list.
    *   **Next Actions:** The single next step for each project goes on a context-based list (e.g., `@Computer`, `@Home`, `@Calls`).
    *   **Calendar:** If it has to be done on a specific day or time, put it on the calendar.
    *   **Waiting For:** If you've delegated something or are waiting for a response, track it on a "Waiting For" list.

4.  **Reflect (or Review):**
    *   Look at your lists regularly. Do a quick **Daily Review** to decide what to do today.
    *   Do a more thorough **Weekly Review** to get clear, get current, and get creative. This is the most critical step for keeping the system functional.

5.  **Engage (or Do):**
    *   With a clear and current system, you can trust your choices about what to work on. You can choose your next action based on context, time available, energy available, and priority.

### Pros and Cons

| Advantages ✅ | Disadvantages ❌ |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Reduces Mental Clutter:** By capturing everything in a trusted system, GTD creates a "mind like water," reducing the stress of trying to remember everything. | **Can Be Overwhelming to Set Up:** The initial "capture" and organization phase can be time-consuming and feel daunting. |
| **Highly Comprehensive:** It's a full life-management system that can handle everything from small to-dos to large, complex projects. | **Lacks a Built-in Prioritization System:** GTD tells you *what* your work is but doesn't offer a strong opinion on *what* to do first. It must be combined with other prioritization methods. |
| **Flexible and Tool-Agnostic:** GTD can be implemented with anything from a simple notebook to sophisticated software. | **Requires Strict Discipline:** The system only works if you consistently capture and review your tasks. The Weekly Review is essential and easy to skip. |
| **Promotes Proactive Engagement:** It forces you to think about the "next action" for every commitment, breaking down procrastination. | **Context-based lists can be less useful** for people who do most of their work in one location (e.g., at their computer). |
