# Belief and Assessment Biases

These cognitive biases distort how we form beliefs, assess evidence, and perceive reality. They affect our judgment by leading us to see patterns where none exist, ignore key information, and cling to our beliefs even when they are wrong.

| Bias | Description | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Association Fallacy** | Incorrectly linking unrelated things because they appear together. | Assuming a person who likes video games must be lazy due to a stereotype. |
| **Conjunction Fallacy** | Believing specific conditions are more probable than a single general one. | Thinking "Linda is a bank teller and a feminist" is more likely than "Linda is a bank teller." |
| **Belief Bias** | Judging an argument's strength by its believability, not its logic. | Accepting a logically invalid argument because the conclusion seems true. |
| **Observer Effect** | Behavior changes when people know they’re being watched. | Employees working harder when their boss is in the room. |
| **Hindsight Bias** | Believing one “knew it all along” after an outcome is known. | Claiming you predicted the winner of a match after the fact. |
| **Attitude Polarization**| Opinions become more extreme after exposure to the same evidence. | Political groups becoming more divided after reading the same news story. |
| **Belief Perseverance**| Clinging to beliefs even when faced with contradictory evidence. | Still believing a debunked conspiracy theory despite evidence. |
| **Representativeness Heuristic** | Judging likelihood by resemblance to a stereotype, not probability. | Assuming a quiet person who loves books is more likely a librarian than a salesperson. |
| **Availability Heuristic** | Estimating probability based on how easily examples come to mind. | Fearing shark attacks because they are vivid in the news, despite being rare. |
| **Anchoring Heuristic** | Relying too heavily on an initial value (the "anchor"). | A $1000 price tag makes a $500 item seem cheap in comparison. |
| **Clustering Illusion** | Seeing patterns in random data. | Believing you're on a "winning streak" in a game of chance. |
| **Gambler's Fallacy** | Believing past random events influence future ones. | Thinking a coin is "due" for heads after several tails. |
| **Rosy Retrospection** | Remembering the past as better than it actually was. | Recalling school years as carefree, forgetting the stress. |
| **Base Rate Neglect** | Ignoring statistical base rates in favor of specific, anecdotal information. | Fearing a rare disease you read about more than a common one like the flu. |
| **Peak-End Rule** | Judging an experience by its peak and its end, not the total sum. | Remembering a vacation by its most exciting moment and the pleasant final day. |
| **Baader-Meinhof Phenomenon** | Noticing something for the first time, then seeing it everywhere. | Learning a new word and then hearing it multiple times that week. |
| **G.I. Joe Fallacy**| Believing that knowing about a bias is enough to overcome it. | Thinking awareness of procrastination prevents it, yet still procrastinating. |
