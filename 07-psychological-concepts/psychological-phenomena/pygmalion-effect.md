# The Pygmalion Effect: The Power of High Expectations

The **Pygmalion Effect**, also known as the **Rosenthal Effect**, is a psychological phenomenon in which higher expectations placed upon people lead to an increase in their performance. It is a form of **self-fulfilling prophecy.**

The effect is named after the Greek myth of Pygmalion, a sculptor who fell in love with a statue he had carved, and his love brought it to life. In the same way, a leader's or teacher's belief in a person's potential can "bring that potential to life."

> "When we expect certain behaviors of others, we are likely to act in ways that make the expected behavior more likely to occur." - Rosenthal & Babad, 1985

---

### How It Works: The Four Factors

Research has shown that when we hold high expectations for someone, we subconsciously change our behavior toward them in four key ways:

1.  **Climate:** We create a warmer, more positive socio-emotional climate for them. We are nicer, more attentive, and more encouraging.
2.  **Input:** We teach them more material and more challenging material.
3.  **Output:** We give them more opportunities to respond, ask more questions, and give them more time to speak.
4.  **Feedback:** We give them more detailed and constructive feedback on their performance. We praise their successes and help them learn from their failures.

This differential treatment boosts the person's self-esteem and self-efficacy, which in turn motivates them to work harder and perform better, thus fulfilling the original expectation.

---

### The Golem Effect: The Dark Side of Expectations

The opposite of the Pygmalion Effect is the **Golem Effect**, where low expectations lead to a *decrease* in performance.

If a manager or teacher believes someone is incompetent, they are likely to:
*   Provide less support and encouragement.
*   Assign less challenging work.
*   Give less feedback.
*   Interpret mistakes as confirmation of the person's lack of ability.

This creates a negative cycle of underperformance and reinforces the person's (and the manager's) belief in their own inadequacy.

---

### Applications and Implications

*   **In Leadership:** The most effective leaders are those who genuinely believe in their team's potential. By setting high but achievable standards and providing the support to meet them, they can elevate the performance of their entire team.
*   **In Education:** A teacher's expectations for a student can have a profound impact on their academic achievement. This is why it's so critical to challenge any biases that might lead to lower expectations for certain students.
*   **In Parenting:** A parent's belief in their child's abilities shapes the child's own self-concept and ambition.

### How to Leverage the Pygmalion Effect

*   **As a Leader:**
    *   **Examine your expectations:** Be aware of your own biases. Do you hold higher expectations for some team members than others? Why?
    *   **Set high but realistic standards:** Challenge your team, but ensure they have the resources and support to succeed.
    *   **Communicate your belief:** Explicitly tell your team members that you believe in them and their ability to succeed.
*   **For Yourself (Self-Pygmalion):**
    *   Set high personal expectations for yourself.
    *   Surround yourself with people who believe in you and your potential.
    *   Use positive self-talk and affirmations to build your own self-efficacy.

The Pygmalion Effect is a powerful reminder that our beliefs about others are not just passive observations; they are active forces that can shape reality.
