# The Golem Effect: The Danger of Low Expectations

The **Golem Effect** is a psychological phenomenon in which lower expectations placed upon individuals lead to poorer performance. It is the negative twin of the **Pygmalion Effect** and a powerful demonstration of a **self-fulfilling prophecy.**

The name comes from the Golem of Jewish folklore, a clay figure brought to life that could become a destructive monster. In the same way, negative expectations can "create" a low-performing, disengaged individual.

---

### How It Works: A Vicious Cycle

The Golem Effect is triggered when a person in a position of authority (like a manager, teacher, or parent) holds low expectations for someone. This belief, often subconscious and based on bias, leads to behaviors that sabotage the individual's performance:

1.  **Less Input:** The authority figure provides less information, fewer resources, and less challenging assignments, assuming the person "can't handle it."
2.  **Less Support (Climate):** The socio-emotional climate is colder. There is less encouragement, less positive feedback, and more criticism.
3.  **Fewer Opportunities (Output):** The individual is given fewer chances to speak, contribute, or take on responsibility.
4.  **Biased Feedback:** Mistakes are seen as confirmation of the person's incompetence, while successes may be dismissed as luck.

This treatment erodes the individual's self-confidence and motivation. They begin to internalize the belief that they are incapable, leading them to disengage, put in less effort, and ultimately perform poorly - thus "confirming" the original low expectation.

---

### Golem vs. Pygmalion

| **Pygmalion Effect (High Expectations)** | **Golem Effect (Low Expectations)** |
| :--- | :--- |
| "I believe in you, you can do this." | "I'm not sure you're up for this." |
| **Leads to:** More support, challenging work, positive feedback. | **Leads to:** Less support, simple work, negative or no feedback. |
| **Result:** Increased confidence, motivation, and **higher performance**. | **Result:** Decreased confidence, disengagement, and **lower performance**. |

---

### Where the Golem Effect Appears

*   **In the Workplace:** A manager who believes a new hire from a non-traditional background is "not a good fit" may unconsciously give them less mentoring and simpler tasks, leading the new hire to become disengaged and underperform.
*   **In the Classroom:** A teacher who believes, due to bias, that students from a certain neighborhood are less capable may call on them less and grade them more harshly, leading those students to fall behind.
*   **In Parenting:** A parent who constantly tells their child, "You're just not good at math," may create a math anxiety that causes the child to avoid the subject and perform poorly.

### How to Prevent the Golem Effect

As a leader, teacher, or parent, preventing the Golem Effect requires conscious and deliberate effort.

*   **Challenge Your Biases:** Actively question your own assumptions about people. Are your expectations based on objective performance or on a subjective "feeling" or stereotype?
*   **Set High Standards for Everyone:** Hold consistently high but realistic expectations for all members of your team or family. Believe that everyone is capable of growth.
*   **Provide Equal Opportunities:** Ensure that everyone has access to the same challenging assignments, development opportunities, and chances to prove themselves.
*   **Focus on Growth, Not Just Performance:** When a mistake is made, frame it as a learning opportunity, not as a confirmation of inability. Adopt a **Growth Mindset**.
*   **Be a Source of Encouragement:** Your belief in someone can be a powerful antidote to their own self-doubt. Look for opportunities to provide genuine, specific praise for effort and progress.

The Golem Effect is a powerful reminder that our expectations don't just predict the future; they help create it. As leaders, we have a profound responsibility to wield that power wisely.
